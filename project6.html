Plastic to Power - Microwave Energy for Plastic Decomposition

Project Overview

This study investigates the use of microwave-assisted catalytic pyrolysis of plastics for hydrogen production, with a focus on microplasmas and hotspot identification within microwave cavities. Using two microwave technologies, an inverter and a transformer microwave, the study explores how energy distribution influences the efficiency and reproducibility of hydrogen production. The research involves thermal mapping experiments to identify energy hotspots, and hydrogen production tests using an FeAl2O3 catalyst under various conditions.

Objectives

  • Investigate whether microplasma formation is the primary heating mechanism in Microwave-Assisted Pyrolysis (MAP) using FeAl2O3 as a catalyst.
  • Explore the use of thermal mapping to identify reproducible hotspots within the microwave cavity and assess their effect on hydrogen production efficiency.
  • Examine the impact of different microwave technologies (inverter and transformer microwaves) on energy distribution and hydrogen production yield.
  • Enhance the understanding of microwave-assisted catalytic reactions for hydrogen production by analyzing the role of microplasmas and energy distribution.
  • Address the challenges of reproducibility in hydrogen production and identify optimal conditions for catalyst performance.

Project Details

The project encompasses the following tasks:

  1. Literature Review: Conduct an exhaustive review of existing research to understand the fundamentals of microwave-assisted catalytic pyrolysis (MAP) and the current landscape of investigations, including the role of microplasmas and energy distribution within microwave cavities.
  2. Temperature Sensing Module Design: Design a system to record video footage and measure temperature using appropriate sensors to capture the thermal behaviour during the pyrolysis process.
  3. Thermal Mapping Test: Perform thermal mapping experiments to identify heating patterns in the microwave cavity and assess the effect of a mode stirrer on the energy distribution and temperature hotspots.
  4. Catalyst Testing and Mapping: Conduct tests using catalysts to map their properties and understand their behaviour under different microwave heating conditions, focusing on hydrogen production efficiency.
  5. Hydrogen Production Testing: Record successful hydrogen production using the microwave-assisted pyrolytic setup and analyse whether the type of microwave technology (inverter vs. transformer) impacts the yield and consistency of hydrogen production.

Results

The study demonstrated that placing the FeAl2O3 catalyst in identified microwave hotspots significantly enhanced hydrogen production, particularly in initial tests with the inverter microwave. This supports the hypothesis that hotspot placement improves the efficiency of microwave-assisted hydrogen production. However, successive tests showed a decline in hydrogen yield, attributed to catalyst exhaustion. Tests with the transformer microwave produced no hydrogen, likely due to the depleted catalyst rather than differences in microwave technology. Equipment constraints, including glassware failure at higher power levels, and limited catalyst availability hindered further comparisons of inverter and transformer microwave performance. The findings emphasise the need for fresh catalyst material to maintain consistent hydrogen yields. They also highlight opportunities for future research, including catalyst regeneration methods, enhanced temperature measurement techniques, and improving material durability for high-temperature microwave reactions. Investigating the role of microplasmas as a heating mechanism remains a critical area for further exploration.
